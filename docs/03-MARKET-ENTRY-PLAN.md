Executive Summary

This plan outlines a strategy to enter the West Palm Beach / Palm Beach
County condo association market with a web and mobile app platform that
helps associations achieve compliance with Florida's expanded website
mandate while providing ongoing operational value. The approach
leverages a regulatory tailwind, a demo-first sales strategy, and
recurring monthly revenue.

However, this plan also identifies significant competitive threats,
structural risks, and strategic choices that must be addressed before
committing resources. The condo compliance website space is not empty
--- multiple established players already serve this exact market. Your
differentiation must be clear, defensible, and articulated from day one.

1\. Market Analysis

1.1 The Regulatory Tailwind

association managing 25 or more units (excluding timeshares) to operate
a compliant website or mobile app. This expands the existing 150+ unit
mandate to cover the vast majority of condo communities in the state.

2\. Differentiation Strategy

Given the competitive landscape, a compliance-website-only play is
insufficient. You need a differentiation angle that the template-based
providers cannot match. Here are the viable options, ranked by
defensibility:

2.1 The Mobile App Angle (Recommended Primary Differentiator)

Most compliance providers offer web portals only. The Florida statute
explicitly permits compliance via a mobile application as an alternative
to a website. A native or near-native mobile app gives you:

-   Push notifications for meeting notices, maintenance updates, and
    payment reminders (critical for engagement --- email open rates for
    condo communications average under 30%)

-   In-app maintenance request submission with photo/video upload

-   Mobile-first document access (residents can pull up bylaws or
    budgets from their phone)

-   A tangible product the board can show residents --- "download our
    community app" is a much stronger pitch than "here's a website
    login"

**Why this works:** CondoSites, CONDUU, and most compliance providers do
not offer native mobile apps. They offer responsive websites. A true app
in the App Store / Google Play is a visible, tangible differentiator
that boards can point to and residents actually use.

3\. Demo-First Sales Strategy

3.1 The Demo Template --- What to Build

Your instinct to build a functional demo is correct, but scope it
tightly. The demo should take no more than 2--3 days to build using
Claude Code and should demonstrate exactly these screens:

Web App (Board/Manager Portal)

-   Dashboard: compliance status checklist (green/yellow/red for each
    statutory requirement)

-   Document library with drag-and-drop upload, auto-categorized by
    statute section

-   Owner directory with username/password management

-   Meeting minutes posting workflow (upload → approve → auto-publish
    with 30-day timestamp tracking)

-   Announcement composer with email blast option

-   Basic maintenance request inbox

Mobile App (Resident-Facing)

-   Home screen: community announcements, upcoming meetings

-   Document access (bylaws, minutes, financials --- pulled from the
    same backend)

-   Maintenance request submission with photo upload

-   Push notification preferences

-   Contact the board / management

4\. Execution Roadmap

Phase 1: Validate Before Building (Weeks 1--2)

Do NOT build the demo yet. Spend two weeks having conversations first.

1.  Pull the Florida DBPR database of condo associations in Palm Beach
    County. Filter for 25--149 units. This is your target list.

2.  Cross-reference against existing websites. Any association that does
    NOT have a discoverable website is a warm lead --- they are
    currently non-compliant.

3.  Identify 30 associations to contact. Prioritize self-managed condos
    (no management company listed) as they have the most acute need.

4.  Reach out to board presidents directly. The compliance deadline has
    passed --- lead with urgency: "Are you aware your association is
    required to have a compliant website as of January 1st?"

5.  Conduct 10--15 discovery conversations. Ask: What software do you
    currently use? What's your biggest operational headache? Have you
    looked into compliance solutions? What's your monthly technology
    budget?

Phase 2: Build the Demo (Weeks 3--4)

Based on what you heard in Phase 1, build the demo. Prioritize the
features that came up most in conversations.

1.  Build the web portal (Next.js or similar) with the compliance
    checklist, document library, and owner portal.

2.  Build a React Native mobile app shell with the resident-facing
    screens.

3.  Deploy to a demo environment with the fictional "Palm Gardens" data.

4.  Prepare a 15-minute demo script that walks through: compliance
    problem → your solution → mobile app differentiator → pricing.

5.  Create a one-page PDF leave-behind with compliance checklist,
    pricing, and your contact info.

Phase 3: Sales Push (Weeks 5--8)

1.  Schedule in-person demos with the associations from your target
    list. Board meetings are ideal --- you present to all
    decision-makers at once.

2.  Attend the next Palm Beach County CAI (Community Associations
    Institute) chapter event. These are networking goldmines for meeting
    board members and property managers.

3.  Contact the property management companies listed in Section 2.2.
    Offer a free pilot for one of their communities.

4.  Target 5 signed contracts in the first month of sales activity.

5.  Use early customers as case studies immediately --- "Tropical Shores
    Condominiums achieved full compliance in 7 days."

Phase 4: Scale and Retain (Months 3--6)

1.  Productize: Move from custom deployments to a multi-tenant SaaS
    architecture so onboarding new communities is fast.

2.  Upsell: Once a community is on the compliance tier, introduce the
    add-on modules (e-voting, payments, etc.).

3.  Referral program: Offer one month free for any board member who
    refers another community that signs up.

4.  Content marketing: Write blog posts about Florida condo compliance.
    Target keywords like "Florida condo website requirement 2026" ---
    this is exactly what panicking board members are Googling right now.

5.  Expand geography: Once the platform is proven in Palm Beach County,
    expand to Broward and Miami-Dade.

5\. Critical Risks & Devil's Advocate Assessment

**Keeping the skeptical board member hat on:** here are the risks that
could kill this venture, along with honest assessments of each.

Risk 1: You're Late to the Party

The January 1, 2026 deadline has already passed. CondoSites has been
operating since 2005 and has been marketing the expanded mandate since
mid-2024. Many associations that were going to act proactively have
already chosen a provider. You're selling to the procrastinators and the
confused --- which is actually a real segment, but they're also the
hardest to close because they're the least motivated.

**Mitigation:** Lead with enforcement consequences. The Division of
Florida Condominiums can take administrative action. Unit owners can sue
for injunctive relief and courts can award \$50/day in minimum damages
for records violations. The "scare" angle works here because it's
factually accurate.

Risk 2: Race to the Bottom on Pricing

CondoSites charges \$55--70/month with zero setup. If a board is
comparing you to them purely on compliance, you lose on price. Your
\$99/mo + \$500 setup is nearly double the cost of the established
player.

**Mitigation:** Never compete on compliance alone. Your pitch is
"compliance is table stakes --- we also give you a mobile app that
residents actually use and a platform that replaces your
email-and-spreadsheet operations." If a board only wants a checkbox
compliance website, let CondoSites have them.

Risk 3: Condo Boards Are Slow Decision Makers

Board decisions typically require a quorum vote. Boards meet monthly.
Getting on the agenda, presenting, getting a vote, and executing a
contract can take 60--90 days. This means your sales cycle is 2--3
months minimum per deal. Can you sustain that cash flow gap?

**Mitigation:** Target self-managed condos where the board president IS
the decision maker. Also target property managers who can deploy across
multiple communities without individual board votes.

Risk 4: Maintenance Scope Creep

Florida condo law changes frequently. HB 913 in 2025 added meeting video
links and expanded document lists. Every legislative session could add
new requirements. Your "maintenance" fee now includes ongoing statutory
compliance updates --- which is development work, not just hosting.

**Mitigation:** Define in contracts exactly what maintenance includes
(hosting, security patches, statutory compliance updates) versus what
constitutes a new feature request (billed separately). Build your
platform to be modular so statutory updates are configuration changes,
not rewrites.

Risk 5: You're a Single Point of Failure

If you get sick, go on vacation, or decide to pivot, your clients'
legally-mandated websites go down. Boards will ask about this during
sales. CondoSites and Condo Control have teams. You have you.

**Mitigation:** Use managed hosting (AWS, Vercel, etc.) so the platform
runs without you touching it. Automate everything possible. Build toward
hiring a part-time support person once you hit 15--20 clients. Be
transparent about your architecture in sales conversations.
