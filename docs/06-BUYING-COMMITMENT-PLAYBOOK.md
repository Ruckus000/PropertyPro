# SECTION 3: BUYING COMMITMENT ASK & TURNAROUND PLAYBOOK

> **How this section relates to Sections 1 and 2:** Sections 1 and 2 build the micro-commitment chain â€” the sequence of small yeses that move a prospect from cold contact through a 14-day audit (board channel) or pilot (PM channel) to the point where you ask for the buying commitment. This section covers what happens at that final ask â€” and, more critically, what to do when the answer isn't an immediate yes.

> **Two different frameworks, two different stages.** The RBO Turnaround Playbook (the separate prospecting document) uses Blount's **Ledge â†’ Disrupt â†’ Ask** framework for *prospecting* objections â€” the brush-offs and reflex responses you get during cold outreach. This section uses Blount's **Relate â†’ Isolate â†’ Clarify â†’ Minimize â†’ Ask** (RICMA) framework for *buying commitment* objections â€” the substantive, genuine concerns that surface when you ask someone to sign an agreement and spend money. These are fundamentally different types of resistance. Prospecting objections are about getting attention. Buying commitment objections are about overcoming real fear, budget concerns, and organizational politics.

> **The cardinal rule:** Never leave a buying commitment conversation without either (a) a signed agreement, (b) a specific date and action for the next step toward signing, or (c) a clear understanding that the deal is dead and should exit the pipeline. "I'll think about it" with no date is not an outcome â€” it's a stall, and stalls kill deals.

## The RICMA Framework â€” How It Works

Buying commitment objections require more depth than prospecting turnarounds. You can't just Ledge-Disrupt-Ask your way through "It's too expensive" when someone is deciding whether to spend their association's money. Blount's five-step framework gives you the structure:

| Step | What You Do | Why It Works |
|------|------------|-------------|
| **RELATE** | Acknowledge the objection with genuine empathy. Don't argue, don't minimize, don't rush past it. Make them feel heard. | Lowers emotional resistance. The prospect expects you to push back â€” when you don't, their guard drops. *(Blount, Sales EQ: "When people feel that you get them, they are far more inclined to accept your ideas.")* |
| **ISOLATE** | Ask: "Other than [this concern], is there anything else that would prevent us from moving forward?" | Surfaces hidden objections. If the stated objection is a smokescreen for a deeper concern, this is where it comes out. If it's the only concern, you now have a bounded problem to solve. |
| **CLARIFY** | Ask follow-up questions to understand the real concern behind the stated objection. "Help me understand â€” when you say it's too expensive, are you comparing it to a specific alternative, or is it that the overall budget can't absorb any new expense right now?" | The stated objection is rarely the real objection. "Too expensive" might mean "I don't think the board will approve it" or "CondoSites is cheaper" or "I don't see enough value for the price." You can't address what you don't understand. |
| **MINIMIZE** | Use their own words â€” the yeses they've given you throughout the process â€” to reframe the objection in context. "You told me during our first call that your board spends 15 hours a month on administrative work. During the audit, your compliance score went from 35% to 88%. Three of your owners logged in within the first week. The question isn't whether the platform works â€” it's whether $199 a month is worth getting those 15 hours back and staying out of legal trouble." | This is where the collected yeses from the audit/pilot period pay off. You're not arguing â€” you're reminding them of their own conclusions. *(Blount, Objections: "The Minimize step is where you gently remind the stakeholder of the commitments they've already made.")* |
| **ASK** | Make the ask again â€” clearly, specifically, and assumptively. If the objection has been genuinely resolved, ask for the commitment. If it hasn't, ask for a fallback micro-commitment that keeps the deal alive. | You've earned the right to ask again. Don't leave the conversation without a concrete outcome. |

**The Fallback:** If the full RICMA turnaround doesn't produce a yes, you always have a fallback â€” a smaller commitment that keeps the deal moving. The fallback is never "I'll follow up next week." It is always a specific, scheduled action with a date.

### SCENARIO 1: Asking the Board President 1-on-1 to Recommend Purchase to the Full Board

**When this happens:** Step 4 â€” Audit Review meeting (Day 12â€“14). The president used the platform during the 14-day audit. You've reviewed the data. Now you need the president to champion a board vote.

**The transition (from data review to ask):**

*"[Name], let me ask you a direct question. Wait for the answer. If yes, continue. If equivocal, probe: *"What part of the problem doesn't it solve?"*

**The ask:**

*"Good. Here's what I'd recommend. Your next board meeting is [date]. I'd like you to put this on the agenda â€” not as a discussion item, but as a motion to approve. Pause. Let them process.

*"Can we get this on the [date] agenda as a motion to approve?"*

**What to do immediately after they say yes:**

1. Confirm the board meeting date and time
2. Ask: *"Who are the other board members, and is there anyone who you think will be a hard sell? I want to make sure the materials address their specific concerns."*
3. Build the mutual action plan (Section 1, Step 4): specific dates for delivering board materials, prep call before the meeting, the meeting itself, and post-vote contract execution
4. Send the mutual action plan within 24 hours

**What to do if they say "I'll bring it up but I'm not sure about putting it to a vote":**

*"I understand the instinct to introduce it as a discussion first. But here's what I've seen happen: when it's a discussion item, the board agrees it's worth looking into, and then it sits on the 'things we should explore' list for three months. When it's a motion to approve, they have a decision to make â€” and they have the data to make it. Your audit results give them everything they need. ## PART B: BUYING COMMITMENT OBJECTION TURNAROUNDS â€” BOARD CHANNEL

### BOARD OBJECTION 1: "We need to think about it."

**What they say:** *"This looks good, but we need to think about it."* / *"Let us sleep on it."* / *"We're not ready to decide today."*

**What they usually mean:** They're not sold on the value, they're afraid of making a wrong decision, or they don't want to be the person who championed an expense that residents complain about. This is rarely about needing more information â€” it's about needing more confidence.

**The underlying concern:** Fear of making a commitment they can't easily reverse. Status quo bias: doing nothing feels safer than doing something.

**RELATE:** *"I completely understand â€” this is an important decision for your community, and you should feel confident about it."*

**ISOLATE:** *"Other than wanting time to think, is there anything specific about the platform, the pricing, or the approach that's giving you pause?"*

*[Listen carefully. If they name something specific, address that instead. If they say "No, we just need time," continue.]*

**CLARIFY:** *"Help me understand â€” when you say you want to think about it, is there a specific question you'd want answered, or is it more about making sure this is the right move for [Association Name]?"*

**FALLBACK:** If they won't commit to a decision date: *"At minimum, can I send you a one-page summary of your audit results to review? ## PART D: PRE-MORTEM â€” OBJECTIONS YOU'RE CREATING YOURSELF

### Objections You're Creating in the Board Channel

**1. "We need to think about it" â€” caused by not collecting enough yeses during the audit.**

If the board president barely used the platform during the 14-day audit, they don't have enough personal experience to feel confident recommending it. The "think about it" objection at the close is almost always a symptom of weak engagement during the audit period. The fix isn't a better turnaround â€” it's a better audit. If the president hasn't uploaded a document, posted an announcement, or seen a maintenance request come through by Day 7, escalate immediately (see Section 1, Step 3 audit protocols).

**2. "It's too expensive" â€” caused by not establishing the cost of the status quo early enough.**

If you wait until the close to discuss pricing, the prospect compares your price to $0 (doing nothing) or to their vague memory of what CondoSites charges. The fix: quantify their current cost in time during the combined discovery/demo meeting (Step 2). Ask: *"How many hours per month does your board spend on document management, meeting notices, and maintenance requests?"* Write down the answer. Use it at the close: *"You said 15 hours a month. At $199/month, the platform costs less than $14 per hour of board time saved."*

**3. "CondoSites is cheaper" â€” caused by not differentiating during the demo.**

If you demo the compliance checklist and the document library but don't show the mobile app, push notifications, maintenance request workflow, and announcement system, the prospect concludes you're selling the same thing as CondoSites for twice the price. The fix: the demo must clearly show the five things CondoSites doesn't offer (mobile app, push notifications, maintenance tracking, compliance deadline monitoring, and meeting notice automation). **4. "We need to discuss this as a board" / "Let's table it" â€” caused by not arming the president as a champion.**

If you have a great audit but the president walks into the board meeting without materials, talking points, or a clear recommendation, the agenda item becomes "the president thinks we should look at a compliance platform" â€” which gets tabled. The fix: the mutual action plan (Section 1, Step 4) and the prep call 3â€“5 days before the board meeting. Never let a champion walk into a committee meeting unarmed.

**5. "What happens if you go out of business?" â€” caused by not addressing trust proactively.**

If the prospect has to raise this concern, you waited too long. The fix: during the combined discovery/demo meeting, proactively mention your hosting infrastructure and data portability before they ask. *"By the way â€” your platform runs on AWS, the same infrastructure as Netflix. Your data is always exportable, and I guarantee full data portability in the agreement."* When they hear it unprompted, it builds trust. When they have to ask, it signals doubt.

**6. Stalled deals in general â€” caused by not scheduling next steps before ending conversations.**

Every conversation that ends without a specific, calendar-locked next step is a deal that will stall. You know this intellectually, but in the moment â€” especially with a polite, friendly board president who says "I'll be in touch" â€” it's easy to let it slide. ### Objections You're Creating in the PM Channel

**7. "I can't add another tool" â€” caused by not demonstrating time savings with their own data during the pilot.**

If you demo the platform at the single-community level without showing the PM dashboard, the portfolio compliance overview, and the time-savings math across all their communities, the PM evaluates $149/month in isolation. **10. "I need to see it work for 6 months" / "Can I keep the pilot going?" â€” caused by not establishing a hard end date with consequences from Day 1.**
